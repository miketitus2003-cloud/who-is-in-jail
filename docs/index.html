Who Is in Jail —
And Why?

By Michael Parham  ·  April 2026

When I was 13 years old, the police knocked on my door at 4:30 in the morning on a school night.

They had pictures pulled from my mother's Facebook account. They treated me like a criminal for something I had nothing to do with.

I was lucky. My parents were home. They were educated. They knew their rights. They told the police they could not search my room. The officers left.

That night, nothing happened to me.

But I never stopped thinking about the kids who didn't have parents at home when that knock came. The kids who didn't know they could say no. The adults who couldn't say no either — because no one ever taught them they had that right, or because the officers didn't care.

The police don't always have the story right. The system is not neutral. It does not weigh everyone equally.

This project is about making that visible — with real data, from the real jails, about real people who are still sitting in there right now.

Question 1

How many people in these jails have never been convicted of anything?

The majority of people in America's largest jails — Rikers Island, LA County Jail, DC Jail, Baltimore City Detention Center — have not been convicted of anything.

They are legally innocent. They are there because they are poor.

A person with money gets arrested and goes home the same night. A person without money gets arrested and sits in a cage — sometimes for months, sometimes for years — waiting for a trial that may never come, or that eventually clears them of everything.

While they wait, they lose their job. Their apartment. Their children.

Same charge. Same bail. One person walks out. The other loses everything — before a jury ever hears a word of their case.

Question 2

How many people pleaded guilty to something they didn't do — because they couldn't afford to wait?

Then they're offered a deal: plead guilty right now and go home today. Or stay here and wait — and risk a worse sentence if you lose at trial.

Most people take the deal.

Not because they're guilty. Because the alternative is unbearable.

This is not justice. This is coercion by poverty.

The longer someone sits in pretrial detention, the higher the chance they plead guilty. Studies show that after 30 days detained, guilty plea rates climb sharply — even for people who were later acquitted or had charges dropped.

8.6×

The system calls it "due process." What it actually is: punishment before trial, used as leverage to extract guilty pleas from people who can't afford to fight back.

The people this hurts most are the people in the poorest ZIP codes — where a $500 bail is not a minor inconvenience. It's an impossible number.

Same $500. Completely different number of days of your life.

Question 3

How many people are locked up for addiction instead of getting treatment?

A massive portion of the people in these facilities are there for drug possession. Not dealing. Not violence.

Possession means addiction in most cases. Addiction is a medical condition.

We lock people up for it instead of treating them.

Criminal Possession — Controlled Substance Possession of Heroin Possession of Cocaine Possession of Fentanyl Drug Paraphernalia Public Intoxication Fare Evasion Trespassing Loitering Petit Larceny (under $250) Sleeping in Public Bench Warrant — Failure to Appear

Then we give them a criminal record that makes it nearly impossible to get housing, employment, or stability when they get out — which makes relapse more likely, not less.

The data shows the cycle. The same people appear in these records over and over, on the same charges. Not because they're bad people. Because they needed treatment and got handcuffs instead.

Arrests for addiction happen in the poorest places. Not because addiction is more common there — but because in wealthier neighborhoods, you get a ride to a facility. In poor neighborhoods, you get a squad car.

Question 4

What are the conditions inside — for people who haven't been convicted of anything?

Rikers Island has been under federal investigation for years. People have died there waiting for trial — on charges that were later dropped.

The violence. The medical neglect. The solitary confinement of people who haven't been convicted of anything. These aren't accidents. They aren't edge cases.

This is the system operating exactly as it was designed — against people who have no power to push back.

17
people died at Rikers Island in 2021 — a record. Most were pretrial.
110%
capacity: Rikers regularly holds more people than it was built for

LA County Jail is one of the largest jail systems in the world. It has a documented history of deputy gangs — groups of officers with matching tattoos who beat detainees and are protected by the institution.

DC abolished cash bail for most offenses in 1992. It shows what the alternative looks like. People go home while they wait for trial. They don't lose their jobs. They don't lose their apartments. The sky did not fall.

These are not isolated incidents. This is what happens when you build a system designed to warehouse poor people and give it almost no accountability.

Question 5

Did the 2017 Maryland Bail Reform actually help — or did the system just adapt?

In July 2017, Maryland implemented sweeping bail reform — moving away from cash bail toward a risk-assessment model. On paper, it was a major change.

The question this project asks: who actually benefited?

When cash bail goes down but remand rates go up by the same amount, the system adapted. The name changed. The outcome didn't.

When reform helps people with resources to navigate a new system — but the poorest ZIP codes see no improvement — reform failed the people who needed it most.

The Vera Institute's county data shows Maryland's pretrial population trends before and after the reform date. The arc is visible. The questions are still being answered.

How This Was Built

The data behind this investigation

Every number on this page comes from public records and verified data sources. No figures were invented. Where data is missing, that gap is documented — not hidden.

Sources used:

NYC DOC Open Data Dataset 7479-ugqb — Daily Inmates In Custody. Live, updated daily. Verified active 2026.
NYC DOC Inmate Discharges Dataset 94ri-3ium — Admission and discharge dates back to 2018. Calculates actual detention length.
Vera Institute — Incarceration Trends County-level jail data for every US county, 1970–2022. The historical backbone. NYC boroughs, LA County, DC, Baltimore City, Prince George's County.
Marshall Project — Deaths in Custody Individual-level records of people who died in custody. Includes pretrial status.
US Census ACS 5-Year Estimates ZIP-level median income, poverty rate, rent burden, household structure. The denominator in the work-days-to-bail calculation.
LA / DC / Maryland individual records Not publicly available via API. FOIA request templates are in the repository. These gaps are documented, not papered over.

Privacy: No names are stored or published anywhere in this project. Every individual identifier is replaced with a one-way cryptographic hash before any data is saved. Exact ages are replaced with 10-year buckets. The raw data never appears in this repository.

The people behind the numbers

Every row in this dataset is a person.

Someone's parent. Someone's child. Someone who woke up one morning and by that night was sitting in a cage — not because they were convicted of anything, but because they were poor, or sick, or in the wrong place, or the police had a story that wasn't fully true.

I know what it felt like to be 13 years old at 4:30 in the morning looking at police officers who had already made up their minds about me. I had parents who could protect me that night.

Most people don't.

This project exists to make that visible. Because the first step to changing something is being honest about what it is.

— Michael
